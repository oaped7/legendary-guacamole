Benguet Cultural Dances



Bendian Dance

Origin: Kabayan, Benguet

Source: Encyclopedia of Benguet Indigenous Knowledge Systems

The Bendiyan is an Ibaloi dance that originated in Kabayan, Benguet. It forms an important part of the Chungas ritual, a ceremony historically performed after a successful head-taking raid. The term bendiyan comes from the Ibaloi word bedey, meaning untiring or doing something to the point of exhaustion. It was believed that anyone who participated in the Bendiyan would never tire during the dance.

Over time, the Bendiyan evolved from a ceremonial practice to a staged performance. Originally a victory dance, it later became associated with healing rituals and prayers for agricultural prosperity, and today, it is also performed to promote cultural appreciation and tourism in Kabayan.

Historical Background

The last recorded celebration of the original Bendiyan with an actual human head took place around 1851 to 1871. According to oral tradition, a warrior named Peang beheaded an important man from Sablan as part of a challenge.

By 1910, Bendiyan was performed without warriors or human heads, marking the beginning of its transformation. The final Bendiyan rites performed as community rituals occurred in Itogon in 1982 and Bocod in 1983, with only a single stanza of the angbawas chant remaining.

Three Variations of Bendiyan

The transformation of Bendiyan resulted in three distinct variations:

1. Original Bendiyan (Discontinued)
  • Performed when warriors returned with an enemy's head.
  • The community celebrated with continuous dancing, followed by rituals the next day.
2. Modified Bendiyan (Most Authentic Contemporary Form)
  • Performed when a descendant of a warrior knight becomes ill or when blessings were needed.
  • Consists of eight stages: background of the tradition, mission, kill, return, identification, rejoicing, thanksgiving, and vigilance.
  • This form is now rare and slowly disappearing.
3. Staged Bendiyan (Performance Bendiyan)
  • A shortened version performed today for cultural events, festivals, and tourism.
  • Focuses on the Sedsed dance, leadership display, and community participation.

Ritual Practices

Traditionally, the celebration begins with the butchering of a pig on the first evening. Gongs and drums are played throughout the night until the next day.

On the second day, the Sedsed dance begins, historically led by three warriors called the Ulol, with the Kabasilja as the leader. Several pigs are butchered and shared among participants. The third day marks the end of the ritual.

Symbolism in the Dance

  • Arms raised with fingers wiggling downward represent rice panicles heavy with grains.
  • Arms lifted upward signify prayers to Kabunyan (native god) and Afu'sheeyus (Christian God) for abundant harvest and blessings.
  • Community unity: The community dances close together, expressing unity and wishing for plentiful rice yields, summarized by the saying: "Say unbusbos dja shili e paguey" – So that each rice stalk will yield many grains

Basic Eight Steps of the Bendiyan

  1. Pinesbekan - Slant
  2. Salawasaw - Present
  3. Kinitangan - Relax
  4. Kinikiyan - Scratch
  1. Inushongan - Look Down
  2. Inudiyan - Reverse
  3. Pinadyosan - Alignment
  4. Inabaya - Catch

Tayaw Dance

Origin: Ibaloi, Kankanaey, and Kalanguya Indigenous Groups of Benguet

Source: Encyclopedia of Benguet Indigenous Knowledge Systems

The Tayaw, also known as the bird dance, is a general term for festival dances performed during various significant events such as weddings, thanksgiving ceremonies, sharing of blessings, death anniversaries, births, curing rituals, and other communal celebrations. Although many associate it mainly with the Cañao (community feast) as a form of joyous merrymaking, the Tayaw is actually an essential and meaningful part of nearly every ceremony among the Ibaloi, Kankanaey, and Kalanguya peoples of Benguet.

The musical accompaniment begins before the first dancer enters the space. A man draped with blankets over both shoulders steps to the center, arms outstretched in imitation of a bird in flight. He moves by hopping, skipping, and walking in rhythmic patterns, occasionally lowering his arms. He is then joined by a woman, who wears her blanket wrapped around her torso. They circle the dance area together until the mambunong (ritual priest) offers the basabas (ritual prayer), during which the dancers pause. After the first prayer, the dance continues until the final basabas, marking the end of the Tayaw. The mangangsa (main gong player) then passes the blankets to the next pair of dancers.

Kinds of Tayaw

1. Sagausau and Ritual Tayaw

A form of Tayaw performed by a male dancer to a faster beat. The movements involve higher leaps and livelier steps.

2. Binukaw

Meaning "like the movement of a hawk searching for prey," this dance is performed particularly when the menedjaw (a respected male leader or elder) is honored. Two menesung (female dancers) and one male dancer participate. The women enter from opposite sides, meet at the center, then follow the male dancer in a counterclockwise direction. Ordinary blankets are used in this dance.

3. Kedaring

An earlier variation of the Tayaw performed during the Aremag ritual to honor deceased ancestors. The name is associated with the karing (brass bracelets) worn by women dancers. It uses the same melody and general steps as the regular Tayaw but requires at least five pairs of dancers. Blankets are placed in a kiyad or labba (winnower basket) for the dancers to use.

The dancers move in circles: men forming the outer clockwise circle and women the inner counterclockwise circle, symbolizing good ancestral spirits. A smaller circle represents spirits of individuals who died by suicide, reflecting the community's traditional view on such deaths.

4. Ginalding

A Tayaw dance performed around the sacrificial pig. It follows the same steps as the regular Tayaw, but the prayers said before and after differ based on the ritual's purpose.

5. Batbat Tayaw (Curing Ritual Tayaw)

This Tayaw is divided into two parts: the first part is danced only by the host family, and the second part is open to the whole community. The transition is marked when the mambunong drapes a cloth over his body.

Music and Performance

The instruments used include:

  • Gangsa/Kalsa (flat gongs)
  • Pinsak
  • Solibao and Kimbal (drums)
  • Tik-tik (percussion bars)

The gong and tik-tik players usually dance behind the main dancers, while the drum players remain at the outer edge of the circle. Elders are given priority in handling the gongs as a sign of respect, and blankets used in the dance are likewise first offered to them.

Sacred Blankets

Ordinary blankets are commonly used for Tayaw. However, during special feasts such as the Peshit, specific blankets such as the Shindi/Chinde are used. These blankets are reserved for the baknang (wealthy or respected families) and are worn by dancers of matching social standing.

Palis Dance

Source: Encyclopedia of Benguet Indigenous Knowledge Systems

The Palis Dance, also referred to as the "Witch Dance," is a ritual dance of the Kankanaey people of Benguet. It is performed during the Palis Ritual, which is believed to counteract acts of witchcraft and to help cure spiritual or psychological disturbances. The ritual calls upon ancestral and spiritual forces to protect and heal the afflicted individual.

Steps in the Palis Ritual

1. Sacrificial Offering

The ritual begins with the sacrifice of a dog, which is offered for the healing ceremony. Two mambunong (ritual priests) stay inside the house with the patient. The first mambunong examines the liver and bile of the sacrificed animal to determine the cause of the ailment, then recites the angba (ritual chant).

2. Healing Prayer and Anointing

The mambunong anoints the patient's knees and feet with the animal's blood while continuously chanting prayers for healing and protection.

3. Musical Signal

Outside the house, a helper plays either a tuguitug or toltol, both bamboo percussion instruments. This serves as a signal for the second high priest to begin the Palis Dance.

4. Beginning of the Dance

The dancing priest wears a balongosong, a crown-like headpiece decorated with chicken feathers. He picks up sharp ritual objects—usually a bolo and a spear—from a kiyag or labba (winnower basket).

He dances by hopping and forcefully stomping on the ground, circling the patient while shouting loudly to repel the malevolent spirit believed to cause the affliction.

5. Chasing Away the Spirit

The dancer then runs around the house, striking the spear against the bolo to symbolize the driving away of the evil spirit. Meanwhile, the second mambunong continues chanting the angba inside.

6. Final Act

The patient is brought outside to sit facing the doorway of the house. The dancer continues his movements, accompanied by the shouting of onlookers.

Traditionally, the dancer eventually replaces the bolo with the head of the sacrificial dog, holding it in one hand while still wielding the spear in the other. He points the spear into the air, circles wildly, and finally strikes the house door with the spear before scattering wood ashes onto the spot. This act signifies the expulsion of the harmful spirit and the conclusion of the ritual.

Musical Instruments

  • Tuguitug / Toltol - bamboo percussion instruments
  • Bamboo drum
  • Spear and dog's head - symbolic ritual instruments

Cultural Significance

The Palis Dance is considered a sacred healing practice rather than a performance dance. Every movement, object, and sound during the ritual is believed to hold spiritual meaning, aiming to restore the well-being of the patient and the harmony of the community.

Taychek Dance

Origin: Karao People of Bokod, Benguet

Source: Encyclopedia of Benguet Indigenous Knowledge Systems

The Taychek is a traditional dance of the Karao people performed during celebrations of successful headhunting in ancient times, abundant rice harvests, and thanksgiving occasions. It is an important part of the Babeng ritual—similar to the Ibaloi Peshit, a communal thanksgiving feast. During Babeng, host families share their blessings not only with relatives but also with neighbors and guests from other communities.

The dance and its rituals reflect the Karao's cultural history, particularly their past traditions of warfare and unity. Today, the Taychek is regarded as both a victory dance and a ritual offering for harvest and prosperity.

Musical Accompaniment

The dance is accompanied by traditional instruments such as:

  • Salaksak
  • Pitot
  • Maliyok
  • Dolon
  • Benengbeng

Male dancers typically play the gongs while the women harmonize their steps to the rhythm.

Performers and Formation

  • Female dancers are paired with male gong players.
  • Another pair acts as Chaligchig (escort dancers), who direct and signal the progression of the dance.
  • Performers hold a tobacco pouch while dancing; smoking is traditionally done after the dance while others continue the performance.

The dance begins with the men and women forming two facing lines. The men beat the gongs while the women match their marching steps to the rhythm. The Chaligchig dancers move forward, turn to face the women, lift their gongs, and hop toward them. At the command "Hoooo-ho!", they lower the gongs and bow.

This sequence is repeated until the dancers tire, and replacements may join at any time.

Cultural Significance

The Taychek is unique to the Karao and is not widely performed outside the community. This is unlike the Bendiyan, which has become more commonly performed in festivals. The Taychek holds deep ritual importance and must only be danced after the required ceremonies are completed. Violating these ritual steps is traditionally believed to bring misfortune.

This sacred nature explains why the Taychek is not widely known to many.

The Taychek and the related Kolimbet dance were once discontinued but were later revived for cultural preservation and community pride.

Clarification of Terms

There has been some confusion regarding the terms Taychek, Itundak, and Tinebdeb-ak. While some Karao people use these words interchangeably, the accepted understanding is:

The official name of the dance is Taychek.

Two Rhythmic Patterns or Musical Beats:
  1. Itundak
  2. Tinebdeb-ak

Kolimbet Dance

Source: Encyclopedia of Benguet Indigenous Knowledge Systems

The Kolimbet is a traditional Karao healing dance performed during the Batbat, a ritual conducted as a form of thanksgiving to the ancestors or deities by the host family. It is also performed during the Ibumangi ritual, which serves as an etong (offering) made to appease the spirits of the dead and grant their requests.

The dance is believed to aid in healing illnesses and acts as a cleansing process for the bereaved family and those who come to offer sympathy and support.

Performance of the Kolimbet

The Kolimbet is performed inside the house, and exclusively by one male and one female dancer. The pair dances in circles around the sacrificial boar. After several rounds, the female dancer dips a blade of grass into a basin of water and swings it gently around while a madmad (ritual prayer) is recited.

This act symbolizes cleansing, healing, and communication with the ancestors. The dance and the sprinkling of water may be repeated several times according to the ritual's needs.

Types of Kolimbet

There are two types of Kolimbet based on the time of performance and ritual purpose:

Type Time Purpose
Kaakawan During the day Performed as a thanksgiving dance
Kalebian Performed at night - nine months after burial Part of the continuing ritual observance and remembrance of the deceased

Ritual Restrictions and Cultural Preservation

Because of panijow (taboos or restrictions), only a few I-Karao elders and ritual practitioners know how to properly perform the Kolimbet. Children are not allowed to handle the musical instruments or participate in the ritual performance. As a result, most young people learn about the Kolimbet only through school presentations or local cultural festivals, where the dance is adapted for educational and cultural awareness purposes.

Despite having simple dance steps, opportunities to practice the Kolimbet remain limited, making its continuation heavily dependent on the elders who still know the ritual.

Tallak Dance

Origin: Bakun, Benguet

Source: Encyclopedia of Benguet Indigenous Knowledge Systems

The Tallak was originally an instrumental device used by the Bakun Kankanaey community long before the introduction of gongs. Its primary function was to keep night guards awake and alert against possible enemy attacks. It also served as a warning signal to alert the community in times of disturbance or infiltration.

As warfare and night guarding became practices of the past, the Tallak transitioned into an instrument used for celebration and communal gathering, particularly during ritual festivities held on summer nights. Over time, simple dance movements were added to accompany the music produced by the Tallak ensemble, eventually forming what is now known as the Tallak Dance.

Performers and Musical Ensemble

The Tallak dance is performed by nine participants, consisting of:

  • Seven Tallak instrument players
  • Two main dancers (one male and one female)

The Tallak ensemble includes the following wooden instruments:

Solnog (lead instrument), Pistan, Pidua, Pangibang, Kalalok, Talok, and Pingsan.

These instruments come in varying sizes and lengths, producing distinct tones when struck using a pitog (wooden stick).

Dance Formation and Steps

  • The instrument players form a circle, creating the outer ring.
  • The main dancers perform inside the circle.
  • The Solnog (lead instrument) signals the start of the dance.
  • Seven or eight secondary dancers may join in the outer circle, moving in harmony with the rhythm.
  • After one full round of movement, the principal dancers enter the center.

The male dancer moves with controlled yet expressive motions, arms extended sideways at shoulder level, matching the cadence of the Tallak instruments. The female dancer complements his movement through synchronized rhythmic steps and graceful posture.

Cultural Significance

The Tallak Dance serves as a reminder of the community's history of unity, alertness, and shared responsibility, while also celebrating joy, togetherness, and cultural identity. Today, it continues to be performed during festivals and cultural presentations to honor the heritage of the Bakun Kankanaey people.

Binegbegan Dance

Source: Encyclopedia of Benguet Indigenous Knowledge Systems

The Binegbegan is a Kankanaey dance performed during the Pedit, a grand feast traditionally hosted by a wealthy family. This dance is derived from the Tallak dance but is slower, more refined, and has more graceful footwork.

The dance is led by a male and female main dancer who move in skipping steps around the dancing area, forming circles, semi-circles, or moving freely in any direction. At times, the main dancers weave among the gong players to showcase their steps. The bouncing or jumping motion distinguishes the Binegbegan from the Tallak dance.

During evening celebrations, multiple pairs—often two or three—may dance at the same time, each pair trying to surpass the others in skill and elegance. When performed for formal ritual occasions or cultural presentations, the single-pair version is usually showcased.

The male dancer wears two native blankets draped loosely over his shoulders, while the female dancer has one native blanket wrapped securely around her torso.

Footwork and Dance Steps

1. Sinagudsud
  • Step the right foot forward with the heel raised.
  • Execute small, closed steps while maintaining forward movement.
2. Pinilay
  • Step the right foot forward with heel raised, accompanied by bouncing knee motions.
  • Heels go down and up with a heavy step, either foot may lead.
3. Lineclecuab
  • A ball-heel step pattern done forward or sideward, then repeated back-to-back in place.
4. Linakik-e
  • Step-hop-step movement used to form a circular formation.
5. Payadpad
  • Done by the drum beaters: legs are lifted alternately backward (right-left-right) in rhythm.
6. Sadung (Arm Movement)
  • Arms raised in a reverse "T" position, slightly forward.
  • Palms face outward, fingers pointed toward the cheeks.

Musical Instruments

The Binegbegan is accompanied by a full gong and drum ensemble, including:

Instrument Description / Function
Gangsa Main flat gong leading rhythm
Takili Supports the main gong
Bangibang Contrasts or counters the main rhythm
Pista Small gong aiding the main gong
Pidwa Complements the Pista
Pingsan Supports the Bangibang
Second Gangsa Enhances the main gong rhythm
Solibao Drum that controls and regulates the overall tempo

Cultural Significance

The Binegbegan remains one of the culturally significant dances of the Kankanaey, valued not only as a form of artistic expression but also as a celebration of community pride, kinship, and tradition.

Grand March Dance

Origin / Practice: Kapangan, Benguet

Source: Encyclopedia of Benguet Indigenous Knowledge Systems

The Grand March Dance serves as the opening ceremony of a formal ball, where participants enter or promenade around the ballroom in coordinated steps. In Kapangan, Benguet, the Grand March is actively performed in various community celebrations such as the Anitap Festival, weddings, graduations, school programs, Christmas, and New Year gatherings.

It remains a well-practiced social dance in the community.

Cultural Shift and Influences

Over the years, there has been a noticeable shift in dance preference from traditional cultural dances to the Grand March. This change is attributed to several factors, including:

  • Influences of colonization
  • Acculturation and cultural exchange
  • Growing interest in popular culture
  • Lack of available traditional musical instruments
  • Limited transmission of sacred cultural dances, which are often performed only by elders

Because many traditional dances are sacred and require proper ritual knowledge, younger generations have fewer opportunities to learn and perform them. As a result, the Grand March became more accessible and commonly practiced in social gatherings.

Dance Characteristics

  • The steps and patterns of the Grand March are determined by the lead dancers.
  • The duration of the dance depends on:
    • The number of participants
    • The complexity and length of the step sequences chosen by the lead dancers

The Grand March emphasizes unity, coordination, and social participation, making it a favored dance for community celebrations.
